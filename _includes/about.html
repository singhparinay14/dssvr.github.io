Objective


Our project group's objective is to study how religious beliefs and understanding work in Virtual Reality (VR). More specifically, the overarching question is 'What are the potential benefits and drawbacks of digitizing sacred spaces through VR?'. This question is very broad and has many different dimensions, such as: How do people connect to their own beliefs? How do people interact with people with the (mostly) same beliefs? How do people perceive and judge beliefs that are different to their own? Essentially, this boils down to three distinctions, i.e. the personal level, the intrafaith level and the interfaith level. Beyond this, there are several more aspects to consider like, whether we talk about a personal, professional or societal context. With our application we want to provide a way to evaluate and hopefully answer these and possibly more different questions.


Interactions


A big part of our application will focus on how people interact with other people. We aim to evaluate how interaction changes in the VR environment. Do people interact more? Do people interact with people/groups they don't normally interact with? Do the users feel like they communicate on a 'better' level? How does the interaction influence the users' understanding and acceptance of other people's practices and beliefs? Some of these questions are more on the objective while others are purely subjective. When it comes to variables like the 'time spent talking' or 'religions of people a user talks to', we can automatically collect data by measuring times users talked to other people and storing the religions of the users. When it comes to variables like the 'quality of an interaction' or 'acceptance/understanding', people might define "good communication" differently which makes it impossible to collect data automatically. In these cases we need direct and personal feedback from the users.


Knowledge and Engagement


Interaction can also happen between user and environment. More specifically, by using interactive objects, i.e., objects that can be manipulated by the user or by observing 'static' information inside the space. Interestig question arise such as: Does VR transmit the essence and meaning of the space better than on a simple 2D screen? If users are given the opportunity to engage with objects they cannot engage with in realtiy, do the take it? Would people make use of 'supernatural' (e.g., lifting really heavy objects) powers? Does interaction with the VR space positively influence understanding of the topic? For question that ask 'do the users use this?' we can automatically collect data by storing if these objects are used. To answer the question if the VR space provides better understandin, direct user feedback is needed since this is subjective.


Accessibility


One big advantage of VR is that people that are physically, mentally or due to personal reasons not able to visit real-world spaces, most of them are still able to do so in VR. While this obviously improves their possibilities, it might still be interesting in which way the users feel these advantages. Do they feel less lonely than with other digital possibilities (e.g. walking around a 3D space only on a screen)? Do they feel more connected to their own faith through these experiences? Because these aspects are purely subjective, direct user feedback is needed to answer these questions.


Children's Perspective


Another group that might heavily be interested and profit from VR technology are children. In many cases it is hard to motivate children to learn about something because they find it boring or find it hard to concentrate. VR provides the possibility to introduce a higher level of 'playfulness' to the learning process than, e.g., watching a video. The possibility to actually walk around the space and experience objects by actively touching them or even play with them might increase their interest in things. These aspects are mostly subjective as well. To answer these questions we need to define terms like 'better'. It might be defined as 'learns more' but 'learning less but with a deeper understanding' might be a sensible definition as well. A few aspects that can be collected automatically are for example the amount of 'visitation time' vs. 'interaction time' (i.e., did the user just pass time or did they actually interact with other objects/people?) or storing if objects that cannot be interacted with in reality (e.g., a very old book) are interacted with when given the opportunity.


Ethical Considerations


While a lot can be done in VR, maybe not everything should be done. There are members of religious communities that feel very strongly about the physical presence of the sacred spaces and the people. In some parts religion itself might even mandate physical presence for certain occasions/rituals (e.g. the Hajj). Using a virtual environment might not be an adequate substitute for some people. A study could try to answer the question 'Do people accept a VR environment as a substitute for the real-world?'. This of course is subjective and answering the question depends on direct user feedback. Since technology inherently tends to induce very different feelings and opinions throughout different generations, it is important to consider this factor when conducting a user study.


Technical Realization


This might be a less obvious question but during this project we will also aim to answer the question 'How do you digitize sacred spaces in VR from a technical (including design) point of view?'. Especially the aspect of 'realism' has a very wide range in VR.
